Walking to Dinner on the Kumano Kodo

April 25, 2026

Upstairs from the taproom where I first experienced Bierstacheln, or beer-poking, I set my brown ale down on a wooden bench and sit beside the dungeon master. I hand him my character sheets for a satyr fey wanderer. In my mind, I return to a version of the Feywild that blends the Southern Appalachians, where I’m from, with the stark mountains of Japan, where I walked the Kumano Kodo’s Nakahechi Route.

***

The bus followed a river east from Tanabe City, carrying me into the springtime wilderness, where the treetops were dotted with deep evergreen, pale lichen, and pink sakura. While rain and wind buffeted the riverbank, I sat inside the Kumano Kodo Kan Pilgrimage Center, sipping green tea and munching on a pickled plum onigiri I’d packed from the city. Because my accommodation stood alongside the river, I waited for the rain to subside so that I could visit the Takijiri-oji shrine to perform the Shinto rites and obtain a stamp for my collection. With time to spare until check-in, I proceeded up a sharp incline to the next shrine on the trail, which, with the heavy rain, had become a temporary creek. A small hole between boulders, once wedged through, signified that a pilgrim would be spiritually reborn into the sacred realm. As the rain had resumed and my equipment was already becoming saturated, I opted to wait until I returned the next day.

I’m joined by a wizard, a monk, and a warrior at the edge of a verdant forest, where we’ve been summoned by a humanoid raven to learn a forgotten song from a fey court. Our party descends a well that seems of infinite depth, the stone siding blending with mesmeric, rainbow light. Down becomes up as we reach the well’s mouth in a different plane, surrounded by a dead forest and a ruined fortress. Perhaps this is the same realm I crossed into on the Kumano Kodo, a realm largely symbolized by Yatagarasu, a three-legged raven whom the gods sent as a guide for Emperor Jimmu when he’d walked the same terrain. The constant changes in elevation led me to misty vistas, followed by a quaint village, Chikatsuyu. My host for the night, an older woman who took care of all her guests, served me shrimp tempura, sashimi, and fried rice. For my neighbors in the dining room, a young couple from Belgium, she’d cooked the fried rice into an omelette as omurice. “Do you speak French or Dutch?” I asked them.

“Dutch, but there’s also the German-speaking part of Belgium too. There aren’t many, but they’re in the east,” they shared. While the couple weren’t hiking the pilgrimage, they had rented a van and were taking it from Hokkaido in the north to Kyushu in the south of Japan.

We cautiously enter the fortress and inspect a dimly lit room to the right, entranced by a large mirror that spans from floor to ceiling. Silhouettes within the mirror conform to our reflections and taunt us. “You’re moving in a loop, working in software and then hiking through nature,” my reflection reminded me as I stared at the sunlit Oto River from the windowed geothermal spring in Kawayu Onsen. Earlier that day, I’d become a Dual Pilgrim and struck a taiko drum at the Kumano Hongū Taisha to acknowledge my calling.

The room locks behind us, and the silhouettes suddenly breach the mirror’s surface, melding into a towering, nightmarish form. I imbue my arrows with psychic damage and fire one at the creature, but it’s reflected back at the monk, crippling her. The wizard summons a firestorm that encircles the creature, burning away its reflective material. As my stomach growls, I recall the grilled ayu and wild boar miso soup, a taste of the rivers and mountains at the Kii Peninsula. The thin slices of boar were tender and gamey, and the savory broth had a subtle sweetness.


With a powerful swing at its lower body, the warrior topples the creature. It falls and shatters. Sheets of paper drift as they slowly fall to the floor. Upon closer inspection, we realize they are the score for the forgotten song. It’s a song I remember from sitting at the ruins of Shingū Castle, looking out at the Kumano River. At the ruins, I sat beneath a leafing camphor tree, watching a black kite circle the summit of the hill. The bird of prey returned to the same branch, only a few yards away. Cherry blossoms bloomed all around, and pergolas bore vines that would soon sprout wisteria. A boatman paddled a crew of pilgrims upstream, away from the Pacific Ocean. What a pleasant day to sit still outside. Further south, fishing boats headed toward the port of Kii-Katsuura, where they brought maguro tuna. I ate this a few days later after visiting the sacred Nachi Waterfall, as a reward for hiking down from the falls to the coastal town with a new friend. Finally, at the sea caves, it was time to settle into the bittersweet fulfillment of a completed journey.

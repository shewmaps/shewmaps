Walking to Dinner on the Camino Portugués

April 24, 2026

My partner and I begin a game of Azul; having been the last to leave Portugal, I take the first turn. Between us sit five cardboard discs, each decorated with a blue, scalloped circle bordered in gold. I’m reminded of the grilled scallops we ordered in Vigo, Spain, the night before we began our walk to Santiago de Compostela.

***

Served on the half-shell at a restaurant near the Concatedral de Santa María de Vigo, the shellfish still carried their orange roe, a soft, briny addition to the sweet flavor of the muscle. These, among other tapas, fueled the chilly morning of my first day’s walking. My partner had begun his pilgrimage a few days earlier. We stopped at a small chapel, its grayish exterior weathered and grimy. A faint orange glow led us inside, where we took a stamp as a memento and proof of passing by. I sat on a pew and took in the atmosphere. Another pilgrim walked to the front and made the signum crucis, or sign of the cross.

We place four tiles atop each disc, randomly drawn from a cloth bag. From one disc, I take two yellow tiles marked with an orange floral pattern and move them to my board, discarding the remaining two tiles to the center pile. The botanical life along the Camino Portugués was as varied as the tiles: oranges and limes, mint and nettles, and eucalyptus and bamboo grew across the Galician countryside. An orange tabby napped on his back between ridges on a terracotta roof. Inspired by the cat’s leisure, my partner and I stopped for a coffee and pastries in a small village before continuing on to Pontevedra.

My partner selects two red tiles from another disc and moves the rest to the center pile. He fills a pattern line with the reds and carefully examines both our boards. We alternate turns until the discs are empty and a center pile remains. In Pontevedra, my partner and I walked along the granite streets until we found a wine bar. Sipping port wine, I asked him if he’d felt anything spiritual on his pilgrimage. “Nothing spiritual, but it’s cool how people come from all over the world. It’s kind of the same as my normal routine: wake up, have coffee and breakfast, do whatever I need to during the day, and relax in the afternoon,” he shared. I was excited for him to experience his first pilgrimage, though he’d had a similarly communal experience on the Milford Track in New Zealand’s Fiordland National Park.

I try to contain my grin with a smirk as I reach for two more yellows from the center pile. I have nowhere on my board to place them except the floor row, where they’ll be deducted from my score this round, but my partner looks flustered. My move leaves my partner with the remaining four reds, which he’ll have to deduct from his score. Maybe he needs to soak in a hot spring at Caldas de Reis, where we spent our third night together on the pilgrimage. Once used for laundry, the open-air pools there are now used for communal relaxing. While he tended to his blisters at Hostal HCELENIS, I soaked my tired feet underneath the blue, cotton-ball sky.


After the round ends and we tally our scores, my partner recovers with a smile. He has arranged his pattern lines in a way that earns bonus points and wins the round. A few rounds later, he wins the game. To celebrate reaching the sprawling, holy site of the Catedral Basílica de Santiago de Compostela in the rain, we dined on bean soup with clams; braised duck leg with charred greens; and toast topped with jamón ibérico and a scallop. We descended below the cathedral’s main altar the next morning to view the relics of Saint James, and afterwards, we attended the Pilgrim’s Mass. Though I’m not a Christian, I couldn’t help but feel spiritually bound to this final moment of the pilgrimage. Yesterday’s rain left us with a meandering walk from the city center to the train station. I asked around for goose barnacles but learned they were out-of-season. Maybe I'll give Galicia another turn.
